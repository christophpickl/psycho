Pygmalion Effect

Definition

High expectations lead to improved performance (and low to worse).

  • The environment (authorities; teachers, managers) create a different context for those with these expectations.

  • The targets internalize the positive (negative) labels.

  • Naming:

    • Greek myth of Pygmalion: A sculptor fell in love with his statue and so it came alive.

    • A.k.a. "Rosenthal effect" after Robert Rosenthal

  • Related to self-fulfilling prophecy.

Teachers were told that randomly selected students were about to experience an intellectual growth spurt. These student actually experienced a significant boost in performance because of the teacher’s expectations.

Figure 1. YouTube Video: "The Pygmalion Effect and the Power of Positive Expectations", 6mins

Four factors influencing the phenomena (teacher’s behavior changed):

  1. Climate: Warmer atmosphere (being nicer) for favorable students.

  2. Input: Teacher give more material to those students.

  3. Response opportunity: Students get more, and longer chances to respond.

  4. Feedback: Students are more praised (positively reinforced) for right answer.
