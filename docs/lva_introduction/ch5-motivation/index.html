Figure 10. The nomenclatur of brain wave types might be a bit confusing: Beta, alpha, theta, delta. More relaxed means slower in frequency and higher in amplitude.
Figure 11. EEG waves of waking and sleeping stages. The brief bursts of rapid waves are called "sleep spindles", marking the onset of sleep. REM sleep (not shown here), looks like beta waves (same as when awake).
Figure 12. We usually go through 4-5 sleep cycles per night, each ending with a REM sleep. Each cycle is lower in depth and REM increases.
Figure 13. The preservation and protection theory states that the major function of sleep is keep us quiet and hidden.
Figure 14. Changes of sleep over life. Sleep time as well as REM decreases when getting older. If the curves were about to be extended to the left (fetus stage), REM sleep would take up most of the day.
Figure 15. It is not sure whether fetuses with age of 7 months dream, but we do know they spend most of their time in REM sleep.
Figure 16. Subjects kept awake for 42 hours, and tested for response time every hour. Even long periods of sleep deprivation, the circadian rhythm still works.
Figure 17. Blue light reset the circadian clock, helping shift workers and world travelers to adapt their sleep schedules.
