4. Conformity Tactics

  1. A _ norm involves perception of which behaviors society approves; a _ norm involves perceptions of how people actually behave.

    1. public; private

    2. private; public

    3. descriptive; injunctive

    4. injunctive; descriptive

      • d.

  2. Most effective in anti-littering behavior?

    1. Showing a video of the importance of cleanliness.

    2. Showing statistical data of the prevalence of littering.

    3. Showing information about the consequence/punishment of littering.

    4. Showing statistical data how cooperative most people are.

      • d.

  3. What’s an example of the "boomerang effect"?

    1. Everyone else is conserving water, thus you take longer showers and don’t worry.

    2. A potential mate is using a reusable cup, you do the same to impress him.

    3. You use more electricity than your neighbours, thus you save some.

    4. All your neighbours are stealing cable TV, you decide to do the same.

      • a.

  4. Foot-in-the-door technique:

    1. Works only when 2nd request comes from the same person as the 1st.

    2. Capitalizes people’s desire for self-consistency.

    3. It’s an example of propaganda.

    4. Works only if request comes from an authority.

      • a.

  5. Door-in-the-face technique:

    1. Example of informational social influence.

    2. Shows people’s desire to be accurate.

    3. Relies a bit on norms of reciprocity.

    4. Works better during a crisis.

      • c.

5. Obedience to Authority

  1. Goal of Milgram’s obedience research?

    1. Abnormal personality characteristics associated with sadistic behavior.

    2. To justify and liberate behaviors linked to genocide and other inhuman acts.

    3. Understand social factors contributing to destructive/immoral behavior.

    4. Cultural differences in aggression.

      • c.

  2. Milgram’s study showed:

    1. Obedience decreased when the experimenter was in the same room.

    2. No participant attempted to end the study, and help the poor learner.

    3. When other "teachers" refused, participants were significantly less obedient.

    4. It was conducted in the 60s and can no longer be replicated.

      • c.

  3. Why did people follow the orders?

    1. They unintentionally adhered to a norm, that didn’t exist, by blindly obeying, but weren’t required.

    2. Caught in an escalation of commitment by increasing shocks in small steps.

    3. The "learner" deserved it.

    4. They didn’t care, just wanted to get the monetary reward.

      • b.

  4. Ethical concern raised?

    1. Compensation was low.

    2. Learned unpleasing things about themselves without agreeing.

    3. Participants could never play the role of the "learner".

    4. They received a 75-volt sample shock before the study.

      • b.

  5. What was the change introduced by the replication decades later (by Mr. Burger)?

    1. Only women.

    2. Stopped at 150 volts.

    3. Told them, study about effects of punishment on learning.

    4. Paid them.

      • b.
